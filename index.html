What we study

All multicellular life evolved from single-celled ancestors, but understanding this transition has been challenging because it happened hundreds of millions of years ago. The MuLTEE overcomes this limitation by evolving multicellularity de novo in the laboratory.

Treatment groups

We maintain three experimental treatments, each with five replicate populations:

Anaerobic (PA1-5)

Populations that cannot respire, grown without oxygen. These populations evolved macroscopic size (visible to the naked eye) and extensive genomic changes including aneuploidy.

Mixotrophic (PM1-5)

Populations grown with glucose as the primary carbon source, capable of both fermentation and respiration. These populations remained microscopic and serve as important controls.

Obligately Aerobic (PO1-5)

Populations grown on glycerol, which can only be respired. These populations showed ecological diversification, with small and large cluster-forming lineages coexisting for over 4,300 generations.

The frozen fossil record

Every ~50 generations (~10 days), we cryogenically preserve all 15 populations. This "frozen fossil record" now contains over 3,000 snapshots, allowing us to resurrect ancestors, replay evolutionary trajectories, and apply new technologies to historical samples.

Current research priorities

  • Origin of multicellular development and cellular differentiation
  • Evolution of reproductive specialization similar to germ-soma differentiation
  • Novel multicellular traits including fluid flow coordination and symmetry-breaking morphologies
  • Changes in underlying cell biology including mitochondrial evolution and whole genome duplication

Resources

The MuLTEE-verse provides open access to our strains, datasets, and protocols. Researchers can request strains from any time point in our frozen archive. All data and analysis pipelines are freely available.

The MuLTEE is conducted at the Ratcliff Lab, Georgia Institute of Technology
