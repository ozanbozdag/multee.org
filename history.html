History of the MuLTEE

From foundational ideas to a multi-decade evolution experiment

Origins (2010-2018): Developing the model system

The Ratcliff Lab spent eight years developing snowflake yeast as a model system for studying the transition to multicellularity. Building on Saccharomyces cerevisiae's genetic tractability, the team evolved and characterized simple multicellular clusters that form through mother-daughter cell adhesion.

Key foundational work by Will Ratcliff and Mike Travisano showed that multicellularity could evolve via mutations in the ACE2 transcription factor, causing daughter cells to remain attached after division. This created branching, snowflake-shaped clusters with an emergent life cycle: groups grow until mechanical strain breaks a cell-cell bond, releasing a clonal propagule.

Intellectual foundations

Several ideas shaped the MuLTEE's design:

JT Bonner's "size comes first" hypothesis proposed that the benefits of early multicellularity stem primarily from increased size. The MuLTEE tests this by selecting for larger clusters through daily settling selection.

Andrew Knoll's work on oxygen and multicellular evolution raised questions about how atmospheric oxygen levels shaped the trajectory of complex life.

Richard Lenski's Long-Term Evolution Experiment with E. coli demonstrated the power of multi-decade experimental evolution. The LTEE inspired the MuLTEE's design: multiple replicate populations, regular cryopreservation, and commitment to truly long-term observation.

Founding the MuLTEE (2018)

When Ozan Bozdag joined the Ratcliff Lab as a postdoc, he proposed evolving snowflake yeast under different oxygen and metabolic conditions as his main project. The positive results from this work, published in Nature Communications (2021), demonstrated that oxygen availability profoundly shapes the evolution of multicellular size. This success transformed the initial postdoc project into the MuLTEE.

G. Ozan Bozdag and William Ratcliff initiated the MuLTEE in early 2018. The experiment began with an isogenic ancestor carrying a clean ACE2 deletion, ensuring all 15 replicate populations started from the same genetic basis.

The selection regime is simple but powerful: daily settling selection favors larger clusters, which sink faster through liquid media. This screens millions of clusters for size each day, efficiently selecting for increased multicellular complexity.

Major milestones

2018

MuLTEE initiated with 15 populations across three treatments

2021

Nature Communications: Oxygen suppression of macroscopic multicellularity (Bozdag et al.)

2023

Nature: De novo evolution of macroscopic multicellularity (Bozdag et al.). Populations evolved 20,000-fold increases in size through "entanglement," becoming as tough as wood.

2024

Nature Ecology & Evolution: Emergence of stable coexistence through niche partitioning (Pineau et al.)

2024

Science Advances: Proteostatic tuning underpins multicellular evolution (Montrose et al.)

Present

Over 9,000 generations completed, with more than 3,000 frozen samples archived

Looking forward

The MuLTEE is designed to run for decades. Our first decadal plan (2025-2035) focuses on the origin of development, reproductive specialization, novel multicellular traits, and how multicellular evolution reshapes underlying cell biology.

The MuLTEE is conducted at the Ratcliff Lab, Georgia Institute of Technology
